Knowledge check

How volcanoes are made

Volcanoes do not appear overnight. They form over thousands of years as molten rock, pressure, and repeated eruptions build a mountain from the inside out.

  1. Magma forms deep underground Heat and pressure melt rock in the mantle or lower crust, especially near plate boundaries and hotspots.
  2. Magma rises through the crust Because magma is less dense than solid rock, it pushes upward through cracks and weak zones.
  3. A magma chamber grows Molten rock pools beneath the surface, collecting gases that increase pressure over time.
  4. The volcano erupts Pressure breaks through the surface, releasing lava, ash, and gas. Each eruption adds new layers to the cone.
  5. The mountain builds up Repeated eruptions stack lava flows and ash layers, slowly shaping the volcano we see above ground.

Safety briefing

Volcano survival guide

Volcanic eruptions can unfold over days or strike with little warning. Follow these field-tested steps to prepare, stay safe, and recover.

Phase 01

Before an eruption

Prepare while the volcano is quiet — planning now saves lives later.

  • Learn your local hazard zones, evacuation routes, and shelter locations.
  • Build an emergency kit: water, food, N95 masks, goggles, flashlight, and radio.
  • Sign up for official alert systems and know how warnings are delivered.
  • Agree on a family meeting point outside the danger zone.
  • Watch for warning signs — increased earthquakes, gas smells, or ground swelling.
Phase 02

During an eruption

Act quickly. Follow official orders and protect yourself from ash, lava, and lahars.

  • Evacuate immediately when authorities issue an order — do not wait.
  • Move uphill and upwind; avoid valleys and river channels where lahars flow.
  • If ash is falling, go indoors, close windows and doors, and stay under cover.
  • Wear an N95 mask or damp cloth over your nose and mouth to filter ash.
  • Do not drive unless necessary — ash clogs engines and reduces visibility.
Phase 03

After an eruption

Dangers remain after the main event. Wait for the all-clear before returning.

  • Return home only when officials say it is safe — lahars can follow heavy rain.
  • Inspect buildings for cracks, gas leaks, and heavy ash on roofs before entering.
  • When cleaning ash, wear a mask and goggles; lightly wet ash before sweeping.
  • Keep away from still-hot lava flows, fissures, and unstable crater rims.
  • Follow updates from local volcanologists — activity can restart without warning.
